A Reflective Essay · Sacred & Statistical

What the Apse
Shares with Expected Goals

On the surprising kinship between church ornaments, Byzantine icons, ceiling frescoes, and the visual language of football data

✦
I · The Opening Reflection

Two Languages Built to Contain the Infinite

Stand inside a Romanesque basilica and tilt your gaze upward. The ceiling blooms with figures — saints with geometric halos, apostles locked in formal symmetry, heavens rendered in ochre and lapis. Now open a football data visualisation on your phone: a pitch map scattered with Voronoi zones, heat gradients, pass networks drawn like constellations. The two images seem to belong to utterly different worlds.

But linger a moment. Both are attempts — sincere, elaborate, obsessive — to map something that resists mapping. The church artist tried to render the divine order of the cosmos. The data analyst tries to render the hidden order underneath ninety chaotic minutes of sport. Both reach for the same thing: a visual grammar capable of holding more truth than raw experience allows us to see.

"Every heat map is a kind of icon. Every icon is, in its own way, a heat map of devotion."

The Invisible Made Visible

This is what unites them most deeply. Medieval worshippers could not see grace, sin, or divine hierarchy — the church's painted ceilings gave those invisible realities a body. Football fans cannot see pressure, compactness, or attacking momentum during a match — data visualisation gives those invisible realities a body too.

III · On Icons Specifically

The Player Radar Chart Is an Icon

Byzantine icons were painted according to strict canons — the proportions of the face, the gesture of the hand, the angle of the gaze were all prescribed by tradition and carried precise meaning. An icon was not a portrait; it was a distilled truth about a person stripped of contingency and accident.

The radar chart used in modern football scouting — that spider-web polygon showing a player's percentile ranking across eight or twelve attributes — follows exactly the same logic. It is not a photograph of a footballer. It is an icon: a formal, conventionalised, tradition-bound image that sacrifices resemblance to reality in favour of essential truth. Messi's radar does not look like Messi. But it tells you something no photograph can.

"An icon was not meant to be realistic. Neither is xG. Both are truer than realism."

The icon's gold leaf background removes the saint from ordinary time — they exist in eternity, outside contingency. The footballer's percentile radar removes them from the noise of individual matches — they exist in the statistical long run, the "true level" that any single game obscures. Both images are acts of theological patience: waiting for the signal beneath the noise.

IV · On Ceiling Frescoes

Match Footage as Narrative Fresco

Ceiling paintings — from the Sistine Chapel to the barrel vaults of village churches in Cappadocia — were always sequential narratives. The Creation. The Fall. The Annunciation. The Passion. They told a story in compressed visual episodes arranged across a continuous surface, meant to be read by moving your gaze across the space.

Football video analysis works in precisely this rhythm. The analyst's cut-up of a match — pressing triggers, counter-press moments, set-piece routines arranged in sequence — is a narrative fresco. Each clip is a panel. The playlist is the vault. The coach walks the players through it the way a medieval priest walked pilgrims through the church's painted walls: this is what happened here, this is what it means, this is what we must understand.

  • 01
    Composition from fragments Fresco cycles compress years of sacred history into one visual field. Match video compresses 90 minutes into 12 focused clips — both are editorial acts that impose narrative on chaos.
  • 02
    The didactic impulse Church paintings were teaching tools for an illiterate population. Tactical video sessions are teaching tools for players who struggle to see patterns in the blur of live play.
  • 03
    The authoritative narrator The priest interpreted the frescoes. The analyst interprets the footage. Both claim access to a level of meaning the congregation cannot access alone — and both must earn that authority.
  • 04
    Repetition as ritual The church calendar repeated the same frescoed stories year after year. Pre-match video routines replay the same tactical themes week after week. Repetition is how abstract truth becomes embodied habit.
V · On Ornaments

Data Tables as Illuminated Borders

Church ornaments — the gilded column capitals, the carved choir stalls, the embroidered vestments covered in vines and beasts — were never merely decorative. Medieval aesthetics held no category of "pure decoration." Every carved leaf contained a moral, every interlaced knot evoked the complexity of Providence. Ornament was argument dressed in beauty.

Look at the elaborate visual design of modern football data dashboards — the custom typefaces, the careful colour grading, the subtle gridlines, the animated transitions in broadcast graphics. These too are not "merely" aesthetic. They are arguments about what football is: a domain serious enough to deserve the visual language of rigor, of science, of authority. The ornament is the claim.

Both traditions also share an anxiety about excess. The Reformation stripped churches of ornament, accusing it of idolatry — of mistaking the image for the truth. There is a parallel reformist movement inside football analytics: statisticians who warn against beautiful but misleading visualisations, who prefer sparse tables to seductive graphics, who fear that the ornament obscures rather than reveals. The arguments are structurally identical across five centuries.

"Every decorated dashboard is, at some level, an act of faith — faith that beauty and truth are not opposed."
VI · Closing Reflection

What This Kinship Asks of Us

The resemblances traced above are not mere intellectual play. They suggest something important about human cognition and human longing. We are pattern-hungry, meaning-hungry animals. Confronted with phenomena too vast or too fast or too sacred to grasp directly — God, or a Pep Guardiola press — we reach instinctively for the same tools: geometry, colour, symbolic convention, narrative sequence, and the ornamental frame that says this matters, look carefully here.

To work in football data analysis with awareness of this tradition is not pretentious. It is humbling. The analyst joins a lineage of people who understood that the gap between raw experience and meaningful understanding is always bridged by images — and that making good images is therefore one of the most serious things a human being can do.

The medieval painter who climbed the scaffold to lay gold leaf on the apse ceiling, and the data scientist who writes the code to render an xG map at match end, are engaged in the same sacred craft: giving the invisible a shape the eye can hold.

Both the church and the stadium are theatres of collective meaning-making. Both need their artists. Both need their analysts. Perhaps they always needed each other.
